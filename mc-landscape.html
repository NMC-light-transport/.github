01 ——

Why Monte Carlo?

Biological tissue is an extraordinarily complex optical medium. Photons launched into skin, brain, or other tissue undergo millions of scattering events before being absorbed, re-emitted, or escaping. Analytical solutions — such as the diffusion approximation or the Kubelka–Munk model — work only in specific regimes.

Monte Carlo simulation treats photon transport as a statistical sampling problem. Each photon packet is tracked step by step: it travels a random path length, scatters at a random angle, and deposits a fraction of its weight. By simulating millions of such packets, accurate fluence distributions, reflectance, and transmittance can be obtained for any geometry and any set of optical properties.

The trade-off is computational cost — which is why the field's history is, in large part, a story of making MC fast enough to be useful (GPU acceleration, parallel computing, variance reduction), and making MC general enough to model new physics such as polarisation, fluorescence, and nonlinear Raman interactions.

The Founding Lineage: MCML (1985–1995)

The canonical biomedical MC code — mcml.c — emerged from a series of contributions between 1985 and 1995, documented in the primary source: Jacques (2022). The paper by Wang, Jacques, and Zheng (1995) has been cited over 4,000 times.

Steven L. Jacques
Father-founder · 1985
Wellman Center MGH → UT MD Anderson → OMLC/OHSU → Univ. Washington
First tissue MC code (1985). HOP/DROP/SPIN/CHECK framework. Supervised Keijzer, Prahl, Wang, Ramella-Roman, Li Ting.
Marleen Keijzer
2nd version · 1989
Delft Univ. → Jacques lab → TU Delft (now Prof., Applied Math)
HOP/DROP/SPIN/CHECK logic (originally in Dutch). Multi-layer aorta model. First fluorescence collection in MC.
Scott A. Prahl
3rd version · 1989
UT Austin (Welch lab) → OMLC → Oregon Institute of Technology (Prof. EERE)
Cartesian coordinates. Henyey–Greenstein analytic sampling. Created omlc.org/software/mc (1996).
Lihong V. Wang
4th version (definitive) · 1992
UTMDACC (Jacques lab) → Washington Univ. St Louis → Caltech (Prof., COIL)
Definitive MCML C code. 173-page manual. First public MC website. First author of canonical 4000-citation paper.

"A key aspect of this effort was the commitment to an open source code and actively sharing the code on websites."

— S.L. Jacques, J. Biomed. Opt. 27(8), 2022

Generation 2: GPU Acceleration & 3D Voxel Codes (2002–2020)

These researchers directly extended MCML and mcxyz.c into new capabilities, forming the bridge between founders and current active groups.

David A. Boas — tMCimg and the 3D Voxel Concept (2002)

David A. Boas at MGH/Harvard published tMCimg: the first publicly available 3D voxel-based MC code, capable of simulating photon migration through complex heterogeneous media including the adult human head. tMCimg is the direct ancestor of both mcxyz.c (2014) and MCX (2009).

Erik Alerstam et al. — First GPU MCML (2008)

Erik Alerstam, Tomas Svensson, and Stefan Andersson-Engels at Lund University published the first CUDA GPU-accelerated version of mcml.c — the proof-of-concept for orders-of-magnitude speedup in tissue MC. This is acknowledged as the first GPU extension by Jacques (2022).

Jessica Ramella-Roman — Polarised MC (2005)

Jessica Ramella-Roman (PhD, Jacques lab, OHSU) created a polarised-light MC code tracking the full Stokes vector. A GPU-accelerated version (pMCX) was later incorporated into MCX by Yan, Jacques, Ramella-Roman, and Fang (2022). She is now a professor at Florida International University.

Alexander Doronin — GPU mcxyz.c (c. 2012)

NMC direct ancestor

Alexander Doronin, as a postdoc in Igor Meglinski's lab at the University of Otago, New Zealand, implemented a CUDA GPU-accelerated version of mcxyz.c and shared it publicly at github.com/aledoronin. This work is explicitly documented in Jacques (2022). Doronin is now a senior lecturer at Victoria University of Wellington — the institutional home of the NMC project.

Li Ting + Yin-chu Chen → mcxyz.c (2014)

Li Ting (Jacques lab postdoc, inspired by Boas's tMCimg) and Yin-chu Chen (Prahl lab graduate student, 2005) wrote separate voxelated versions that were combined into the current mcxyz.c — a 3D voxel code where each voxel can have unique optical properties.

Marti, Andersen & Hansen — MCmatlab (2018)

Dominik Marti, Peter E. Andersen, and Anders K. Hansen at DTU Fotonik (Denmark) developed MCmatlab: a MATLAB-integrated parallel version of mcxyz.c for teaching. It adds a heat diffusion simulator and Arrhenius tissue damage model, runs parallel processors (~8× faster than mcxyz.c on a single CPU), and is explicitly cited by Jacques (2022) as a key extension.

A. Phong Tran — Curved Boundaries in Voxel MC (2020)

A. Phong Tran (graduate student, Fang lab at Northeastern, in collaboration with Jacques) modified mcxyz.c to allow unique refractive indices per voxel, with smooth pre-processed boundaries for correct refraction at oblique interfaces.

Active Platforms Today

MCX / MMC — COTI Lab, Northeastern University
Qianqian Fang (PI) · Boston, USA · mcx.space

The most feature-complete open-source MC platform currently available. Fang earned his PhD at Dartmouth and joined David Boas's lab at MGH Martinos before moving to Northeastern (2013). His 2009 foundational paper demonstrated ~400× speedup over tMCimg.

MCX (CUDA) MCX-CL (OpenCL) MMC (mesh) pMCX (polarised) MCX Cloud fNIRS / brain imaging

Team: Shijie Yan (pMCX), Yaoshen Yuan (MMC), Leiming Yu, A. Phong Tran, Fanny Nina-Paravecino

VTS / MCCL — Beckman Laser Institute, UC Irvine
Vasan Venugopalan (PI) · Irvine, CA, USA · virtualphotonics.org

NIH-funded LAMMP P41 Center. Code originated in 1998 (Dunn & Smithies at BLI). Carole Hayakawa's PhD breakthrough — perturbation and differential MC for real-time sensitivity calculations — became the scientific core. David Cuccia ported to C# (MCCL, 2010).

Perturbation MC Differential MC C# open-source GUI + fibre-probe ATK NIH education program

Key people: Jerome Spanier (co-founder, mathematician; d. 2024), Carole K. Hayakawa (lead dev.), David J. Cuccia (C# port; now Modulim Inc.), Lisa Malenfant

Diffuse Optics & MC Reference — Univ. Florence
Fabrizio Martelli (PI) · Florence, Italy

Unique in bridging analytical diffusion theory with MC validation. Their textbook (Light Propagation through Biological Tissue, SPIE Press, 2009/2022) is the field's primary reference text. Co-organised the JBO "30 Years of Open-Source MC Codes" special section (2022). Recent extension to Raman time-domain modelling in diffusive media.

Diffusion ↔ MC validation MC scaling relations Raman diffuse optics Time-resolved spectroscopy

Collaborators: Angelo Sassaroli (Tufts), Samuele Del Bianco (CNR), Tiziano Binzoni (Geneva), André Liemert (Ulm)

Multi-Scattering — Jönsson & Berrocal, Lund University

Edouard Berrocal (PhD, Cranfield 2006; Div. Combustion Physics, Lund) and Joakim Jönsson developed a free online GPU-accelerated MC platform. It achieves up to 800× acceleration over CPU using four GPU cards, capable of simulating 1 billion photons in 10 seconds at optical depth 10. The Berrocal–Meglinski collaboration (2005) connects this platform's lineage to the Doronin/NMC branch through Igor Meglinski.

The Nonlinear Frontier: Raman & Beyond

Classical MC codes model linear photon transport: absorption, elastic scattering, and (sometimes) fluorescence. The emerging frontier addresses nonlinear and inelastic interactions — particularly Raman scattering, which is key for label-free chemical tissue identification.

Periyasamy & Pramanik — MCML-EO + RMCC
IISc Bangalore / NTU Singapore → Iowa State Univ. (Pramanik, PI)

First group to extend MCML to include embedded geometrical objects (sphere, cylinder, ellipsoid, cuboid) with refractive-index boundaries (MCML-EO, 2014), then add inelastic Raman scattering (RMCC, 2015). Experimentally validated against gelatin tissue phantoms with cuboid inclusions.

MCML-EO (geometry) Raman MC (RMCC) OCT MC
Dumont, Fang & Patil — isoweight Raman MC
Temple University (Patil PI) + Northeastern (Fang, co-author)

Extended Fang's MCX with the "isoweight" approach to Raman scattering, achieving ~100× speedup via GPU parallelisation. Covers 800–2000 cm⁻¹ Raman spectral range. Fang is co-author, making this a direct code-level link between the MCX ecosystem and Raman MC.

isoweight algorithm MCX-based GPU Raman + fluorescence
Akbarzadeh & Leblond — Spectroscopic Raman MC
Polytechnique Montréal (Leblond PI) + CRCHUM

Full spectroscopic MC package combining absorption, fluorescence, elastic scattering, and inelastic Raman in a single framework. Validated against tissue phantoms for depth-resolved Raman sensing. Leblond's group leads in intraoperative Raman guidance for brain and lung cancer surgery.

Full spectroscopic MC Depth-resolved Raman Surgical guidance
Hokr, Yakovlev & Scully — Stimulated Raman MC
Texas A&M University (Scully group)

Published the first time-dependent MC model for stimulated Raman scattering (SRS) in turbid tissue (ACS Photonics, 2014). SRS is a coherent, intensity-dependent nonlinear process — the first genuinely nonlinear interaction incorporated into a tissue MC framework. Yakovlev is now an active collaborator on the NMC project.

SRS MC (first, 2014) Time-dependent MC Nonlinear population transfer NMC collaborator (2023–)

NMC: Position & Contribution

NMC Project

What makes NMC distinct

The NMC project, led by Alexander Doronin at Victoria University of Wellington, occupies a specific and novel position at the nonlinear frontier of biomedical MC simulation:

  1. Unified nonlinear MC framework. NMC handles both spontaneous and stimulated Raman scattering in a single photon transport engine — not as an add-on to a linear code.
  2. Power-efficient computation. The Neu(t)ralMC iteration (Clennell et al. 2023) targets Apple M-series (ARM) processors, enabling high-throughput simulation on low-power hardware without a dedicated NVIDIA GPU.
  3. Verified lineage. Doronin's GPU mcxyz.c (Otago, ~2012) is acknowledged by Jacques (2022) as a key extension of the foundational code. NMC is the natural evolution.
  4. Collaborative nonlinear physics. The Yakovlev collaboration (Texas A&M) — co-author of the first SRS MC paper (2014) — brings the deepest expertise in nonlinear Raman physics into the framework.
  5. Object-oriented architecture. Earlier work (Doronin & Meglinski 2011, 2012) introduced OO and distributed/P2P MC architectures underpinning NMC's modularity.

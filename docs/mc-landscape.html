Why Monte Carlo?

Biological tissue is an extraordinarily complex optical medium. Photons launched into skin, brain, or other tissue undergo millions of scattering events before being absorbed, re-emitted, or escaping. Analytical solutions — the diffusion approximation, the Kubelka–Munk model — work only in specific regimes.

Monte Carlo simulation treats photon transport as a statistical sampling problem. Each photon packet is tracked step by step: it travels a random path length, scatters at a random angle, and deposits a fraction of its weight. By simulating millions of such packets, accurate fluence distributions, reflectance, and transmittance can be obtained for any geometry and any set of optical properties.

The trade-off is computational cost — which is why the field's history is a story of making MC fast enough to be useful (GPU acceleration, parallel computing, variance reduction), and making MC general enough to model new physics: polarisation, fluorescence, and nonlinear Raman interactions.

02 ——

The Founding Lineage: MCML (1985–1995)

Precursor: Brian C. Wilson & G. Adam (University of Toronto) published the first MC model for tissue in 1983 — photon absorption and flux in homogeneous tissue with isotropic scattering. The conceptual breakthrough that established the feasibility of the approach. doi:10.1118/1.595361

The canonical biomedical MC code — mcml.c — emerged from a series of contributions between 1985 and 1995, documented in Jacques (2022). The Wang, Jacques & Zheng (1995) paper has been cited over 4,000 times.

Steven L. Jacques — mcml.c (1985)

In 1985, Steven L. Jacques wrote mcml.c — the first MC code for tissue optics — while working as a research associate at the Wellman Center for Photomedicine, Massachusetts General Hospital, Boston. He implemented a lookup-table approach to encoding various scattering functions and explored their effect on reflectance and transmittance for comparison with experiments on ex vivo skin. His MC simulation sampled an exponential probability for the photon step size.

Marleen Keijzer — HOP / DROP / SPIN / CHECK (1989)

Marleen Keijzer, a graduate student from Delft University working in the Jacques lab, wrote the second version. She introduced the four-step photon logic — HOP, DROP, SPIN, CHECK — that underlies all descendants of mcml.c. She demonstrated the delivery of light into a multi-layered tissue, and in a companion work demonstrated the delivery of excitation light into aorta and the collection of fluorescence from the tissue.

Keijzer M. et al. (1989). Light distributions in artery tissue. Lasers Surg. Med. 9, 148–154.

doi:10.1002/lsm.1900090210

Scott A. Prahl — Variable Stepsize, Mismatched Boundaries, Cartesian Coordinates (1989)

Scott A. Prahl (graduate student, UT Austin, A. J. Welch lab) wrote the third version. A variable-stepsize weighted MC model was implemented. Both mismatched boundary conditions and anisotropic scattering were included. He also simplified the code by using Cartesian coordinates and introduced analytic sampling of the Henyey–Greenstein function. In 1996, Prahl created omlc.org/software/mc — the first public website for sharing the MC code.

Prahl S.A. et al. (1989). A Monte Carlo model of light propagation in tissue. Proc. SPIE IS5.

doi:10.1117/12.2283590

Lihong V. Wang — Definitive MCML (1992)

Lihong V. Wang, postdoc in the Jacques lab at UTMDACC, wrote the fourth and definitive version of mcml.c in 1992. He reorganised the code for multiple planar layers with unique optical properties including refractive index, made it input-file driven, and wrote a 173-page manual (omlc.org/software/mc/man_mcml.pdf). He was first author of the canonical paper.

Wang L.V., Jacques S.L., Zheng L. (1995). MCML — Monte Carlo modeling of photon transport in multi-layered tissues. Comput. Methods Prog. Biomed. 47, 131–146.

doi:10.1016/0169-2607(95)01640-F

Generation 2: GPU Acceleration & 3D Voxel Codes (2002–2020)

Building on MCML, major extensions introduced GPU acceleration, 3D heterogeneous geometry, polarisation, and new code architectures.

Boas — tMCimg (2002)

David A. Boas (MGH/Harvard) published tMCimg: the first 3D voxel-based MC code for heterogeneous media including the human head. Direct ancestor of both mcxyz.c and MCX.

Alerstam et al. — First GPU MCML (2008)

Erik Alerstam, Tomas Svensson, Stefan Andersson-Engels (Lund Univ.) published the first CUDA GPU-accelerated version of mcml.c — the first proof-of-concept for orders-of-magnitude MC speedup. Explicitly cited in Jacques (2022).

Fang — MCX (2009)

Qianqian Fang (PhD, Dartmouth → Boas lab, MGH Martinos → Northeastern Univ.) developed MCX: the most widely adopted GPU-accelerated 3D voxel MC platform. Its voxel architecture builds on Boas's tMCimg concept while the photon physics inherits from MCML. The 2009 paper demonstrated ~400× speedup.

Ramella-Roman — Polarised MC (2005)

Jessica Ramella-Roman (PhD, OHSU, Jacques lab → FIU, Prof.) created a polarised-light MC code tracking the full Stokes vector. A GPU version (pMCX) was later co-developed with Yan, Jacques, and Fang and incorporated into MCX (2022).

Doronin — GPU mcxyz.c (c. 2012)

NMC direct ancestor

Alexander Doronin, postdoc in Igor Meglinski's lab at the University of Otago, implemented a CUDA GPU-accelerated version of mcxyz.c, shared at github.com/aledoronin. Explicitly cited in Jacques (2022). Doronin is now senior lecturer at Victoria University of Wellington — the institutional home of NMC.

Li Ting + Chen → mcxyz.c (2014)

Li Ting (postdoc, Jacques lab, inspired by Boas's tMCimg) and Yin-chu Chen (Prahl lab, 2005) wrote separate voxelated codes combined into mcxyz.c: each voxel can have unique optical properties.

Andersen & Marti, DTU Fotonik — MCmatlab (2018)

Peter E. Andersen, Anders K. Hansen, and Dominik Marti at DTU developed MCmatlab: MATLAB-integrated parallel version of mcxyz.c for teaching, adding heat diffusion and Arrhenius damage modelling. ~8× faster than mcxyz.c on a single CPU. Explicitly cited in Jacques (2022).

Tran — Curved Boundaries (2020)

A. Phong Tran (Fang lab, Northeastern, + Jacques) modified mcxyz.c to allow unique refractive indices per voxel with smooth boundary pre-processing for oblique refraction.

The Nonlinear Frontier: Raman & Beyond

Classical MC codes model linear photon transport: absorption, elastic scattering, and sometimes fluorescence. The emerging frontier addresses nonlinear and inelastic interactions — particularly Raman scattering, which enables label-free chemical tissue identification.

Martelli et al. — Analytical Raman Models (2016–)

Martelli, Binzoni, Del Bianco et al.
Univ. of Florence (UNIFI), Dept. Physics & Astronomy, Italy · Collaborators: Binzoni (Geneva), Liemert (Ulm), Pifferi (Milano), Sassaroli (Tufts)

From 2016 onward, developed time-domain analytical forward solvers for Raman signals in diffusive media — parallelepiped and cylinder geometries — based on coupled diffusion equations at excitation and emission wavelengths. Validated against MC simulations. Unique bridge between analytical theory and MC. Extended to 2-layer media (2025).

Analytical Raman solversTime-domain diffuse RamanMC reference datasets2-layer models

Key works: Martelli et al. Opt. Express 2016; SPIE 2017; Light Propagation through Biological Tissue SPIE Press (2010, 2022 2nd ed.); Photonics 2025.

Periyasamy & Pramanik — MCML-EO + RMCC (2014–2015)

Vijitha Periyasamy & Manojit Pramanik
IISc Bangalore / NTU Singapore → Iowa State Univ. (Pramanik PI)

First group to extend MCML with embedded geometrical objects (sphere, cylinder, ellipsoid, cuboid) — MCML-EO (2014) — and then add inelastic Raman scattering — RMCC (2015). Validated against tissue phantoms. Also extended to OCT MC simulation.

MCML-EO (geometry)Raman MC (RMCC)OCT MC

Key works: J. Biomed. Opt. 19(4) 2014; J. Raman Spectrosc. 46(7) 2015; IEEE Rev. Biomed. Eng. 2017.

Hokr & Yakovlev — Stimulated Raman MC (2014)

Brett H. Hokr, Vladislav V. Yakovlev, Marlan O. Scully
Texas A&M University (Scully group)

Published the first time-dependent MC model for stimulated Raman scattering (SRS) in turbid tissue — the first genuinely nonlinear interaction incorporated into a tissue MC framework. Based on the MCML package. Yakovlev is now an active collaborator on the NMC project (2023–).

SRS MC (first, 2014)Time-dependent MCNonlinear population transferNMC collaborator 2023–

Key works: Hokr, Yakovlev, Scully. ACS Photonics 1(12), 2014.

Krasnikov, Seteikin & Roth — Raman MC (2016–2019)

Ilya Krasnikov, Alexey Seteikin, Bernhard Roth, Merve Meinhardt-Wollweber
Amur State University, Russia + Hannover Centre for Optical Technologies, Leibniz Univ. Hannover, Germany

Developed a series of efficient two-stage MC approaches for Raman scattering in turbid media: first computing photon fluence, then generating Raman photons. Accounts for realistic detector geometry, sampling volume, and internal absorption. Validated for both confocal and fibre-probe setups in homogeneous and multi-layer phantoms.

Key works: JOSA A 33(3) 2016; JOSA A 34(12) 2017; JOSA A 36(5) 2019.

Akbarzadeh, Edjlali & Leblond — Full Spectroscopic MC (2020)

Alireza Akbarzadeh, Ehsan Edjlali, Frédéric Leblond et al.
Polytechnique Montréal + CRCHUM, Montreal, Canada

Developed a full spectroscopic MC package combining absorption, fluorescence, elastic, and inelastic Raman scattering in a single framework. Validated against tissue phantoms for depth-resolved Raman sensing. Leblond's group leads in intraoperative Raman guidance for brain and lung cancer surgery.

Key works: J. Biomed. Opt. 25(10), 2020.

Vladyko & Doronin — NMC (2023–)

NMC
Ilya Vladyko, Alexander Doronin, Vladislav V. Yakovlev
Victoria University of Wellington, NZ + Texas A&M, USA · lighttransport.net

The first unified open-source MC engine for both spontaneous and stimulated Raman scattering in biological tissue. Optimised for Apple M-series (ARM) processors — enabling high-throughput simulation on low-power hardware without a dedicated NVIDIA GPU. The Neu(t)ralMC iteration (2023) provides energy-efficient transport; the 2025 extension advances Raman signal modelling capabilities.

NMC: Position & Contribution

NMC Project

The NMC project, led by Alexander Doronin at Victoria University of Wellington, occupies a unique position at the nonlinear frontier:

  1. Unified nonlinear MC framework. Handles both spontaneous and stimulated Raman scattering in a single engine — not an add-on to a linear code.
  2. Power-efficient computation. Neu(t)ralMC (2023) targets Apple M-series (ARM) processors — high-throughput without a dedicated NVIDIA GPU.
  3. Verified lineage. Doronin's GPU mcxyz.c (~2012) is acknowledged in Jacques (2022). NMC is its direct evolution.
  4. Collaborative nonlinear physics. Partnership with Yakovlev (Texas A&M, first SRS MC paper 2014) brings the deepest available expertise in nonlinear Raman physics.
  5. Object-oriented & P2P architecture. Earlier OO and distributed P2P MC (Doronin & Meglinski 2011, 2012) underpins NMC's modularity.
